Your parent meets Pearl

Pearl introduces herself as a friendly companion — warm, patient, unhurried. She chats about the day, shares little tips, listens. There's no mention of fraud software. That would feel patronising. Pearl is just a friend.

2

Pearl listens for signals

As they talk, Pearl notices the patterns that predict scams: urgency, secrecy, unusual payment requests, unfamiliar "relationships," pressure to act fast. She doesn't accuse — she asks gentle clarifying questions.

3

They can check anything, anytime

Got a strange text? Paste it in. Unsure about an email? Pearl looks at it. Talking to someone online who seems too good to be true? Pearl can help think it through — without judgment, without alarm.

4

The family stays informed

You get a gentle weekly digest — what was checked, any flags raised, overall risk level. If something urgent comes up, you hear about it right away. Your parent knows you care. They don't feel spied on.

5

Money doesn't walk out the door

Before any significant payment, Pearl softly suggests looping in a trusted family member. Not an alarm. Not a lecture. Just: "Have you mentioned this to your daughter? It might be worth a quick call."
